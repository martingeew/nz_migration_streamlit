# AEWV & India Migration Skill Shift — Media Commentary & Analysis

Research date: 2026-06-27

---

## Official data confirmation

India is the #1 source country for AEWV approvals as of February 2025 — 140,257 total AEWVs approved since July 2022, ahead of Philippines and China. This directly corroborates the dashboard finding of India's rising dominance in NZ work migration.

**Note on data comparability:** The 140,257 figure covers AEWV approvals only (one visa subtype). The MBIE skill level dataset used in the dashboard covers all work visa types (~200,000+ approvals per year across all streams). The two cannot be directly reconciled — the MBIE data is the appropriate source for skill-level trend analysis.

The NZ Treasury / Productivity Commission (2024) explicitly noted that temporary migration "has seen a shift towards a greater share of migrants working in less-skilled occupations" throughout the post-2015 period.

## Human Rights Commission review (August 2024)

The most substantive critical commentary is a formal Human Rights Commission review of the AEWV scheme, published August 2024. Key findings:

- Widespread recruitment fraud and exploitation, particularly among Indian, Chinese, and Bangladeshi workers
- Migrant workers described the visa as "handcuffs" — employer-tied structure deters workers from reporting abuse
- ~200 accredited employers had licences suspended or revoked for visa scams
- Recruitment fees of $15,000–$60,000 being charged to migrants before arrival
- "Job tokens" reportedly on-sold for $30,000 by some employers; INZ delays audits for 24 months
- 2024 review emphasised "systemic vulnerabilities" from underfunding of monitoring
- NZ CTU called the system a "free-for-all" and demanded a fundamental overhaul

## BBC / mainstream media — "unsustainable" migration (April 2024)

BBC reported on NZ tightening AEWV rules in April 2024, framing it as a government response to "unsustainable" migration levels. The article noted low-skilled applicants now face English-language requirements and a shorter maximum stay (3 years down from 5). PM Luxon described the inherited system as having "opened the floodgates" and called net migration levels unsustainable.

The April 2024 reforms — adding English requirements, reintroducing advertising requirements, cutting max stays for Level 4-5 workers — map directly onto the structural break visible in the dashboard data.

**Source**
- BBC News: https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/c25l0vxpnd8o

## NZ-India Free Trade Agreement and the "butter chicken tsunami" controversy (2025–2026)

The NZ-India FTA was signed on 27 April 2026, after negotiations launched in March 2025. The deal includes provisions for an annual pathway for Indian skilled workers into NZ. NZ First minister Shane Jones described the migration component as a "butter chicken tsunami," triggering a national and international backlash about racism and attitudes toward the Indian community.

The BBC Asia Specific podcast (1 May 2026) covered the controversy directly, framing Indian migration as the flashpoint in what is otherwise a broadly supported trade deal. Wikipedia notes NZ First voted against the agreement in Parliament, while Labour supported it.

## Policy reform timeline (for dashboard context)

| Date | Event |
|---|---|
| July 2022 | AEWV launched, replacing Essential Skills visa; no Labour Market Test for most roles |
| April 2024 | Reforms: English requirements added for Level 4-5 roles; advertising requirements reintroduced; max stay cut to 3 years for lower-skill roles |
| August 2024 | Human Rights Commission review published — widespread exploitation found |
| December 2024 | Government announces further AEWV reforms (effective 2025) including wage threshold removal |
| March 2025 | NZ-India FTA negotiations launched |
| April 2026 | NZ-India FTA signed; "butter chicken tsunami" controversy erupts |
